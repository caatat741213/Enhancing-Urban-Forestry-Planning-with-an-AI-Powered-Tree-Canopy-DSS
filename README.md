

### Urban Heat Island Effect(Scatter Plot)
* This visualization reveals a strong negative linear correlation between **Canopy Coverage (%)** and **Land Surface Temperature (LST)**. The downward slope indicates that for every percentage increase in canopy cover, the surrounding summer temperature drops significantly.
* **Decision-Making Value:** The Decision Support System (DSS) successfully pinpoints the red **"High Priority Zone"** on the left. This informs city policymakers that any neighborhood with less than 20% canopy coverage faces extreme urban heat island risks (reaching $32^\circ\text{C}$ to $34^\circ\text{C}$). Consequently, the forestry department should mandate that the first wave of tree planting initiatives be deployed directly to the communities within this zone.

### 📊 Critical Tree Maintenance (Bar Chart): 
* This chart identifies the top 5 core census tracts across the Waterloo and Kitchener regions with the highest volume of high-risk, aging trees (**Critical Trees**, $\text{DBH} > 45\text{cm}$).
* **Decision-Making Value:** Historically, the city might have distributed trimming and removal budgets evenly across all districts. This chart directly isolates **Tract 0007.00** (nearly 600 hazardous trees) and **Tract 0003.00** (over 400 hazardous trees). Utilizing this ranking, the DSS automatically generates budget allocation weights—recommending that the city immediately divert 60% of the total budget to prioritize these top two communities. This ensures preventative trimming is completed before the storm season, mitigating the risk of falling trees damaging property.

### 🔵 Social Equity & Tree Distribution (Bubble Chart):
* This dashboard cross-references the **Socioeconomic Vulnerability Index** ($X$-axis) with **Environmental Greenness** ($Y$-axis).
* **Decision-Making Value:** * The **"CRITICAL NEED"** quadrant in the bottom-left exposes a stark **Environmental Injustice**: these communities are socioeconomically disadvantaged, lack canopy cover, and bear the highest temperature burdens (indicated by large red bubbles). 
  * Conversely, the top-right quadrant represents affluent, heavily vegetated areas with abundant environmental resources.
* **DSS Transformation Outcome:** This dashboard empowers the city to move away from biased or arbitrary fund allocation. By using data as a shield, policymakers can confidently prioritize funding and resources for the vulnerable neighborhoods in the bottom-left quadrant, achieving a truly equitable distribution of urban resources.
